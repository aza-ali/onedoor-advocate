# OneDoor, Pitch Script

**Format:** spoken, 3 acts, roughly 3 minutes. Plain delivery. Pause where marked.

---

## ACT 1, The "No" That Was Wrong

[Open cold. No slide. Just say it.]

Every year in California, 2.7 million people are told "no" on food benefits, or never apply at all, because a screener ran the federal default rule and stopped there.

Federal rules are the floor. California stacks its own rules on top: higher income limits, different deductions, different treatment of households. So a tool that screens on the federal default tells a real family "you don't qualify" when, under California's actual rules, they do.

That gap has a price tag. Roughly 3.5 billion dollars in CalFresh benefits go unclaimed in California every year. Money that's already appropriated. Money that's sitting there. Families just never get through the door.

[Pause.]

So the problem isn't that the help doesn't exist. The problem is that the front door reads the wrong rulebook, and tells people to walk away.

---

## ACT 2, The Door Is Getting Worse, Not Better

Here's why this gets urgent in 2026, not someday.

Two things are happening at once.

First, the incumbents are pulling back. In 2025 the GetCalFresh assister path, the guided human-backed lane that walked people through an application, is being sunset. So the most trusted hand-holding option is going away right when people need it most.

Second, the rules underneath everyone's tools are changing on hard dates. H.R.1 in 2026 introduces new seams that a static explainer page gets flat wrong. April 1, eligibility changes for certain immigrant households. June 1, the ABAWD work requirements shift. A blog post or a PDF written last year doesn't know that. It will confidently tell someone the old answer.

[Slow down here.]

And a wrong answer here is not a typo. A wrong "you don't qualify" means a family doesn't eat. A wrong "you do" means they fill out paperwork, get denied, and never trust the system again. On a dated seam, static content doesn't just go stale. It does harm.

So we have a door that's closing, and the few signs left on it are pointing the wrong way.

---

## ACT 3, The Demo

[This is the live part. Be clear about what's real.]

Let me show you OneDoor. I'm going to walk you through Maria.

**Say this out loud:** Maria is a cited composite. She is not a real user. Every fact in her profile maps to a real eligibility rule, but Maria herself is constructed for this demo.

Maria gives us her situation in Spanish, in her own words. Household size, income, who lives with her, her work hours.

[Run it.]

A federal-default screener says: NOT eligible.

OneDoor runs the same facts through our compiled, cited engine, the one that stacks California's actual rules on top of the federal floor. It flips her: CA-ELIGIBLE. And it doesn't just say yes. It gives her a real number. **231 dollars a month.** With a citation next to every rule that got her there.

[Beat.]

Now the honest part, the part most demos hide.

There is one fact the corpus cannot settle on its own. A genuine ambiguity in Maria's case where the documents disagree. Opus catches that. It doesn't guess and move on. It flags it, and on a live, disclosed call to a county benefits line, we resolve it with a human on the record.

And then this. On the way through, Opus made an overconfident assumption earlier in the reasoning. It catches its own mistake and corrects it, out loud, in the trace. That's the difference between a tool that sounds sure and a tool you can trust on a date that matters.

Finally, it preps Maria to walk into the county office: what to bring, what to say, what to expect.

[Stop. Mark the line clearly.]

**Real versus staged, so I'm not lying to you:**
- The compiler is real.
- The eligibility number, the 231 dollars, is real, from the eval.
- The county call is real.
- The in-room coaching, Maria walking into the office, is a **staged preview**. We script that to show you where this goes.

## The Weakness, Named Before You Ask

[Mar Hershenson framing. Say it before they say it.]

The obvious hole: consumer willingness-to-pay here is weak. Maria is not going to pull out a credit card to find out she qualifies for food benefits. So a direct-to-consumer model is dead on arrival.

That's not a problem we're hiding from. That's exactly the reason who-pays is B2B. The payer is the system that already loses when Maria falls through: counties, managed-care plans under CalAIM, the entities on the hook for outcomes and already funded to close these gaps. They pay to get people through the right door. Maria never sees a bill.

[Close.]

The door is closing. The signs are wrong. We built the one that reads California's real rulebook, shows its work, and admits what it doesn't know. That's OneDoor.
