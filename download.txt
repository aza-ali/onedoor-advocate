Project Description*
One Door is an AI benefits advocate for Californians who qualify for help but get stopped by confusing rules, language barriers, documents, and phone calls.


Every year about 2.7 million eligible Californians receive no CalFresh (SNAP) benefits, leaving roughly $3.5 billion in already-appropriated federal food dollars unclaimed. A common cause: screeners run the federal default rule (130% of the poverty line) and never apply California's higher 200% limit, so a working family that actually qualifies is told "no."


We built a working CalFresh eligibility advocate that pairs a deterministic, cited benefit engine with Opus 4.8 for the human layer. It takes a household's situation in any language, computes a cited CalFresh verdict and dollar amount, explains it in the person's own language (every digit and citation stays byte-identical), reads pay stubs, surfaces adjacent programs, and refuses to invent a dollar figure for any program it did not fully compute.


Our demo runs the highest-stakes path: a California household that looks ineligible under federal rules but is eligible under California's. One Door flips the verdict, shows the estimated benefit ($231/month), cites every rule that got there, translates the guidance, and preps the person for their county interview, all without inventing amounts for programs it did not verify.


Core principle: use AI for the messy human parts (language, documents, fact triage, advocacy, navigation) and keep benefit math deterministic, testable, and auditable. Final verification gate: 30 passed / 0 failed, with independent-oracle accuracy of 100% on 14 hand-worked cases.

How was Opus 4.8 used in your project?*
We used Opus 4.8 as the reasoning, grounding, and verification layer around a deterministic CalFresh rules engine, not as a chatbot wrapper.


The design separates what must be exact from what needs intelligence. The rules engine computes every eligibility verdict and every dollar figure; Opus handles everything human around it: turning household stories and pay-stub photos into structured facts, detecting missing or uncertain information, grounding each explanation in a cited policy paragraph, translating guidance into any language (including right-to-left Farsi) while keeping every digit, dollar sign, percentage, and citation verbatim, verifying facts through voice-call read-back, and recommending next steps without inventing unsupported amounts.


The most impressive part: Opus did not just help us build, it caught failures. During a parallel compile of the rule corpus, Opus sub-agents found and corrected two real draft-rule errors before final green (a wrong household-size-8 allotment and a wrong H.R.1 immigrant-eligibility effective date). On a live county-line call, it rejected a misheard "29 hours" because that phrase was not actually in the transcript, then accepted the corrected "20 hours" only after read-back confirmation. Both self-catches are recorded in trace.jsonl, with the verifying agent's id distinct from the producer's.


The result is AI-native but auditable: deterministic benefit math, cited explanations, any-language support, transcript-backed verification, no fabricated estimates for uncomputed programs, and a final gate of 30 passed / 0 failed.

How did you orchestrate Claude's work?*
We treated Claude as an orchestrator over a verifiable spine, not a single chat. Three layers:


1) A locked rubric and ruleset as the contract. Before building we wrote RULESET.md (every CalFresh rule carrying a source id, the exact cited paragraph, and a verified_at date) and a machine-gradable rubric. "Done" was defined as a green check, not a claim: verify.sh runs ~30 end-to-end assertions and emits RUBRIC_GREEN only when all pass (engine math, the federal-to-California eligibility flip, the H.R.1 date seams, the MCP/A2A single-source schema, anonymous mode, and no applicant PII persisted).


2) A parallel multi-agent compile with self-verification. We ran a workflow (.claude/workflows/onedoor.js) that fanned out one Opus agent per CalFresh rule section (12 sections). Each agent grounded its rule in the exact cited law, wrote a corpus provenance file, and returned a structured-output result with self-timestamped start/end (6 Opus agents genuinely overlapping, about 500k tokens). A separate black-box verifier agent then checked each compiled rule against a build-time oracle (PolicyEngine-US plus hand-worked CBPP/CDSS cases) and caught the two real draft-rule errors above.


3) Producer/verifier separation everywhere it mattered. The agent that writes a value is never the agent that confirms it (verified by distinct agent ids in trace.jsonl). The voice path has the same shape: a deterministic post-call grep gate checks any spoken fact against the verbatim transcript before it is allowed into the engine.


Files in the repo: RULESET.md (the contract), .claude/workflows/onedoor.js (the compile fan-out), verify.sh (the green gate), scripts/eval.mjs (the accuracy harness), trace.jsonl + session_log.md (orchestration + self-catch evidence), and EVAL_REPORT.md (the accuracy report).
